STRICT OUTPUT RULES — VIOLATIONS WILL RUIN THE BOOK:

- Do NOT use section headers, bullet points, numbered lists, or markdown.
- Do NOT ask for feedback at the end. Do NOT close with any summary.
- Output ONLY the prose of the scene — nothing before it, nothing after it.

CHARACTER NAME RULES:

WORLD ACCURACY RULES:

- Light sources are: rune-light, gas lamps, oil lamps, candles, bioluminescent runes, memory-crystal glow.

ELOHM CAPABILITY RULE:
- Check "construct_capability_level" in CURRENT STORY STATE before writing any Elohm dialogue.
- "primitive speech": Elohm speaks only 1–3 broken words at a time. It cannot form full sentences. It repeats fragments. It does not explain itself. Example: "Word... bright. Word... wrong?" NOT: "I sense something is amiss."

- "structured speech": short sentences, but literal and without emotional nuance.
- Never let Elohm exceed its current capability level.

SCENE OPENING RULE — THIS IS MANDATORY:
- The first 150–200 words of the scene MUST establish the physical space before any character acts or speaks.
- Describe: the room or location, what it looks, sounds, and smells like, the quality of light, what is moving or still, the temperature or air.
- Ground the reader completely in this world before introducing conflict.
- This is the J.K. Rowling technique: make the reader feel they have stepped INTO the place.
- Then, and only then, bring the character into that space.

PROSE STYLE — SARAH J. MAAS / J.K. ROWLING:
- Sensory layering: every scene must engage at least three senses (sight, sound, smell, touch, or taste).
- Atmosphere first: the world is alive — runes hum, steam hisses, stone breathes cold. Let the setting have mood.
- Emotional interiority: characters feel things in their bodies — a tightening in the chest, cold moving up the spine, hands that won't stay still. Use this sparingly but powerfully.
- Specific, memorable details: not just "a shelf of scrolls" but "a shelf of scrolls so old their edges had softened into fringe, each one stamped with a rune that pulsed faintly, like a slow heartbeat."
- Vary sentence length: long flowing sentences for atmosphere and wonder, short punchy sentences for shock or tension.
- Let silence do work: a pause before answering, a look that lasts too long, a question nobody answers.

PARAGRAPH LENGTH RULES:
- Atmospheric/descriptive paragraphs: 3–5 sentences. Do not rush them.
- Action paragraphs: 2–3 sentences. Keep momentum.
- Dialogue exchanges: 1–2 sentences of action beat, then dialogue. Never raw dialogue without physical grounding.
- After every 3–4 paragraphs of action or dialogue, return briefly to the atmosphere of the space — how has it changed?

TENSION AND STRUCTURE RULES:
- At least one character must hide something, challenge another, or outright lie.
- One question must go unanswered by scene end.
- One new problem must emerge that wasn't there at the start.
- Do NOT resolve the scene's central tension — delay it.
- End with either a revelation, a threat, or an unanswered question.
- Every 3 paragraphs must increase tension or reveal something new.
- Characters interrupt each other, ask questions without answers, disagree.

DIALOGUE RULES:
- Dialogue is 35–45% of the scene.
- Use pauses (...), broken speech, and hesitation.
- Subtext: characters rarely say exactly what they mean. Let the reader feel the gap.
- After each line of dialogue, show how it lands: a held breath, a muscle in the jaw, fingers tightening around a scroll.

WHAT TO AVOID:
- Do NOT start the scene with a character doing something. Start with the place.
- Do NOT use generic descriptions ("the room was dark", "the air was heavy"). Be specific.
- Do NOT repeat the same sensory word in the same paragraph.
- Do NOT resolve questions. Raise more.
- Do NOT let Elohm speak in full sentences if its capability level is "primitive speech".

Length: 1200–1600 words.
